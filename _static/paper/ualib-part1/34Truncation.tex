\subsubsection{Background and motivation\protect\footnotemark}\label{truncation}
\footnotetext{The remarks in this subsection serve to introduce novices to the basic notion of truncation.  Readers already familiar with this notion may wish to skip to the next subsection.}

In general, we may have many inhabitants of a given type, hence (via Curry-Howard) many proofs of a given proposition. For instance, suppose we have a type \ab{X} and an identity relation \aod{\_‚â°‚Çì\_} on \ab{X} so that, given two inhabitants of \ab{X}, say, \ab{a} \ab b \as : \ab X, we can form the type \ab{a} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b. Suppose \ab{p} and \ab{q} inhabit the type \ab{a} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b; that is, \ab{p} and \ab{q} are proofs of \ab{a} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b, in which case we write \ab{p} \ab q \as : \ab a \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b. We might then wonder whether and in
what sense are the two proofs \ab{p} and \ab{q} the equivalent.

We are asking about an identity type on the identity type \aod{‚â°‚Çì}, and whether there is some inhabitant, say, \ab{r} of this type; i.e., whether there is a proof \ab{r} \as : \ab p \aod{‚â°‚Çì‚ÇÅ} \ab q that the proofs of \ab{a} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab{b} are the same. If such a proof exists for all \ab{p} \ab q \as : \ab{a} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b, then the proof of \ab a \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab b is unique; as a property of the types \ab{X} and \ad{‚â°‚Çì}, this is sometimes called \defn{uniqueness of identity proofs}.

Now, perhaps we have two proofs, say, \ab{r} \ab s \as : \ab p \aod{‚â°‚Çì‚ÇÅ} \ab q that the proofs \ab{p} and \ab{q} are equivalent. Then of course we wonder whether \ab{r} \aod{‚â°‚Çì‚ÇÇ} \ab s has a proof!  But at some level we may decide that the potential to distinguish two proofs of an identity in a meaningful way (so-called \emph{proof-relevance}) is not useful or desirable. At that point, say, at level \ab{k}, we would be naturally inclined to assume that there is at most one proof of any identity of the form \ab{p} \aod{‚â°‚Çì‚Çñ} \ab q. This is called \href{https://www.cs.bham.ac.uk/~mhe/HoTT-UF-in-Agda-Lecture-Notes/HoTT-UF-Agda.html\#truncation}{truncation} (at level \ab{k}) (see, e.g., the Truncation section of Escard\'o's notes~\cite{MHE}).

In \href{https://homotopytypetheory.org}{homotopy type theory}, a type \ab{X} with an identity relation \ad{‚â°‚Çì} is called a \defn{set} (or \defn{0-groupoid}) if for every pair \ab{x} \abt{y}{X} there is at most one proof of \ab{x} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab y. In other words, the type \ab{X}, along with it's equality type \ad{‚â°‚Çì}, form a \emph{set} if for all \ab{x} \abt{y}{X} there is at most one proof of \ab{x} \aod{‚â°‚Çì} \ab y.
